# The protocol for becoming a vendor for federal and provincial procurement opportunities

**Prepared:** 2026-06-05 · **For:** Matthew and Russell · **Use:** a short spoken narrative (about
45 seconds), voiced as the Chief Executive Officer of the National Aboriginal Capital Corporations
Association, plus a documented fact-check and review. No em dashes. Sources inline.

---

## The narrative (about 45 seconds, ~115 words)

> We are hearing from more First Nation businesses ready to win government work, and the path is
> clearer than people think.
>
> First, get your business properly registered, federally or provincially. Most vendors incorporate
> as an Ontario or a federal corporation.
>
> Second, register on the federal business directory. There you confirm your business is at least
> fifty-one percent First Nation owned and controlled. You provide proof of status, where you live,
> where you operate, and how to reach you.
>
> Third, join the new First Nation Procurement Authority. It is our own registry, free to First
> Nation businesses, built to verify you once and open the door to federal, provincial, and
> corporate buyers.
>
> Tell us what you sell. We will help you get compliant, registered, and ready to bid.

**Delivery notes.** Spoken at a natural pace this runs about 40 to 48 seconds. The three numbered
steps are the spine; keep them. If you need to trim, cut the last "corporate buyers" clause, not a
step.

## Fact-check and review (the critique with documentation)

Matthew's draft was close on the spirit and right on the headline requirements. Four corrections
below keep it defensible, because a procurement claim that cannot be substantiated carries real
penalties (bid forfeiture, contract termination, future disqualification).
([Office of the Procurement Ombud](https://opo-boa.gc.ca/praapp-prorev/2026/epa-ppr-03-2026-eng.html))

### 1. "Register with NACCA" is not quite the right body

The association is the national network of the lending institutions; it does not itself certify a
business as First Nation for procurement. The body that does is the **First Nation Procurement
Authority**, created May 1, 2025 by five national First Nation economic organizations (the
association is one of the five founders). It runs a single, trusted First Nation business registry
and offers **free** registry and certification to First Nation businesses, with buyers paying for
access. This is the cleanest fit for a First-Nation-only framing, and it is new, so position the
venture as an early registrant.
([NACCA press release](https://nacca.ca/inaugural-board-announces-creation-of-the-first-nations-procurement-authority/),
[IndigenousSME](https://indigenous-sme.ca/unlocking-indigenous-prosperity-through-procurement-the-first-nations-procurement-authority/))

### 2. The federal directory requirement is real, and the documents Matthew listed are right

Registering on the federal business directory requires the business to be **at least 51% owned and
controlled** by a registered person, and to provide proof of identity. A **status card (Indian
registration)** is an accepted proof, along with the owner's information and the business's
location and contact details, which matches Matthew's "status card, residence, where operating,
contact information." So that part is accurate.
([ISC, how to register](https://www.sac-isc.gc.ca/eng/1756215160157/1756215188889),
[Indigenous Chamber of Commerce MB](https://www.indigenouschambermb.ca/2025/07/10/all-you-need-to-know-about-the-indigenous-business-directory-ibd/))

### 3. Incorporation as an Ontario or federal corporation is recommended, not strictly mandatory

This is the main correction. The federal directory accepts **sole proprietorships, partnerships,
bands, cooperatives, not-for-profits, and joint ventures**, not only corporations. So "it must be
an Ontario Inc or a federal corp" overstates it. What is true: **if** the business is a
corporation, it must supply articles of incorporation, a shareholders agreement, and a shareholders
registry showing First Nation owners hold final decision-making control. The narrative above says
"most vendors incorporate" rather than "must," which keeps it honest while still steering Matthew's
preferred path.
([ISC, how to register](https://www.sac-isc.gc.ca/eng/1756215160157/1756215188889),
[Publicus, Certificate of Indigenous Business](https://publicus.ai/glossary/certificate-of-indigenous-business))

### 4. Federal eligibility is self-attested; the directory is for visibility and set-asides

Worth knowing so we do not promise the wrong thing. To bid on a federal set-aside, the business
**self-certifies** that it meets the ownership and control definition (Annex 9.4 of the Supply
Manual, certified under clause A3000T), and must continue to meet it for the life of the contract.
There is no central pre-approval gate, but registering on the directory is how buyers find you and
how you appear in set-aside searches. So the directory is the practical front door even though it is
not a legal precondition to bid.
([ISC, how federal procurement works](https://www.sac-isc.gc.ca/eng/1696513417823/1696513480139),
[Gowling WLG](https://gowlingwlg.com/en/insights-resources/articles/2025/procuring-an-edge-for-indigenous-businesses))

### Two notes on Matthew's constraints

- **The "no Indigenous, First Nation only" wording cannot be total, because the federal department
  and its directory are legally named with that word.** The narrative above avoids it by saying "the
  federal business directory." Just know that on the actual government forms the word appears, and
  the federal directory itself also lists Métis and other businesses. The **First Nation Procurement
  Authority** is the one registry that is First-Nation-specific by design, which is another reason to
  lead with it.
- **"Provincial" needs its own check.** The federal directory and the Procurement Authority are
  federal and corporate channels. Ontario runs its own supply-chain and procurement programs with
  separate vendor registration. The narrative says "federal, provincial, and corporate buyers,"
  which is the ambition; before Matthew says it on the record, confirm the specific Ontario vendor
  path so we are not implying one registration covers the province. (Flagged, not yet verified.)

### Bottom line

The three-step spine (incorporate, register on the federal directory, join the First Nation
Procurement Authority) is sound and well documented. The only claim to soften before recording is
that incorporation is mandatory, and the only claim to verify before recording is that provincial
procurement runs through the same registration.
